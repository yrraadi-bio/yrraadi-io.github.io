Does Muon improve regulatory DNA learning? Part 2.

March 2026 · Akshat Singh, Viraj Doshi

Highlights AdamW = Adam + Weight Decay

AdamH = Adam + Hyperball

In Part 1, we showed that MuonW reaches target validation perplexity with roughly 37% fewer FLOPs than AdamH. We swept five learning rates across four optimizer configurations and compared convergence speed, spectral dynamics, and training stability. We ended with an open question: “does this translate to improvements on tasks that matter for biology, such as transcription factor motif recovery, and sequence generation?”

This post answers that question. We run the same 20-configuration sweep, evaluate generated sequences at every training checkpoint, and measure biology instead of loss. The answer: MuonW’s FLOP efficiency advantage extends directly to motif abundance, reaching biological quality thresholds in roughly half the training epochs. But the story is more nuanced than “Muon wins again” — different biological dimensions have fundamentally different convergence dynamics, and the right optimizer depends on which dimension you care about.

The Loss–Biology Gap

All four optimizer configurations converge to nearly identical training loss (~1.20 per nucleotide) after 20 epochs. Yet when we evaluate the generated sequences against a comprehensive biological quality suite, they produce substantially different results.

Why should identical loss produce different biology? The cross-entropy loss averages over all positions equally, but regulatory function concentrates in the ~10–20bp motif cores that occupy <10% of the sequence. A model that predicts flanking nucleotides perfectly but misses the binding site grammar can still achieve low per-position loss.

The gap between “good at predicting nucleotides” and “good at generating functional regulatory elements” motivates everything that follows. To characterize it properly, we need to sweep the same hyperparameter grid from Part 1 — but evaluate on biology.

The Evaluation Suite

We developed a six-axis evaluation framework, each axis targeting a distinct aspect of regulatory biology:

TF binding grammar measures whether generated sequences preserve the combinatorial logic of transcription factor co-occurrence. We quantify this using the Frobenius distance between reference and generated co-occurrence matrices, and test structural similarity with a Mantel permutation test (10,000 permutations).

Motif abundance and fidelity measures how many TF binding sites are preserved and whether their relative frequencies match the reference. We scan all 16,249 sequences per condition against 22 JASPAR vertebrate motif matrices, computing per-TF hit rates, aggregate cosine similarity, and per-sequence Jensen-Shannon divergence.

Predicted transcriptional activity uses the Malinois MPRA model1Malinois is trained on real MPRA data and predicts log2 fold-change of transcriptional activity from sequence alone. to predict whether generated sequences would drive gene expression in K562, HepG2, and SK-N-SH cell lines.

Cell-type specificity tests whether sequences generated with a K562 prompt contain K562-appropriate TFs and not hepatocyte TFs, using Fisher’s exact tests.

Regulatory element type fidelity tests whether generated promoters look like promoters (high CpG O/E) and enhancers look like enhancers (cell-type-specific TFs).

Sequence diversity measures mode collapse: unique TF binding profiles, pairwise Jaccard similarity, and k-mer diversity.

We evaluate all 20 configurations (4 optimizers × 5 learning rates) plus the reference dataset. All generations use temperature 0.5.

Biological Convergence

In Part 1, we tracked perplexity across training checkpoints to measure convergence speed. Here we do the same for biology. We evaluated generated sequences at every checkpoint (epochs 1–19) for the best learning rate per optimizer, scanning each against 22 JASPAR TF motif matrices and computing both motif abundance (TF hit rate correlation) and TF grammar (co-occurrence Mantel r).

The convergence curves reveal a key asymmetry. Grammar is learned early and is relatively optimizer-insensitive — all four optimizers reach Mantel r ≈ 0.75–0.85 within the first few epochs. The co-occurrence structure of TF binding is apparently one of the first things the model captures, regardless of how its gradients are processed.

Abundance is where the optimizers diverge. MuonW’s rate correlation rises steeply from 0.37 at epoch 2 to 0.81 at epoch 8 — a 2.2× improvement in 6 epochs. AdamW, by contrast, hovers between 0.29 and 0.47 across the entire training run. AdamH improves slowly, reaching its peak (~0.67) only at epoch 14.

This has a direct efficiency implication. If your objective is motif-rich sequence generation, MuonW reaches its biological target in roughly half the training epochs that Adam variants need. In Part 1, we showed MuonW reaches target perplexity with 37% fewer FLOPs. Here the advantage is even larger for motif abundance: MuonW crosses rate correlation 0.70 at epoch 7, while AdamH crosses it at epoch 14 (if at all). Grammar, however, is not a bottleneck for any optimizer — it converges fast across the board.

Biological Learning Rate Sweep

In Part 1, we swept learning rates and plotted perplexity vs LR for each optimizer. Here we do the same, but for biological quality metrics. Each point represents a fully trained model evaluated on the complete biological suite.

Several patterns stand out immediately:

  • MuonW scales with LR on motif abundance. Its rate correlation climbs from 0.621 at lr = \(9 \times 10^{-5}\) to 0.824 at lr = \(3.6 \times 10^{-4}\), and its unique TF profiles nearly double from 928 to 1,689 at the highest LR. This mirrors Part 1’s finding that MuonW “favors scale.”
  • AdamH peaks in the mid-range for grammar. Its Mantel r reaches 0.775 at lr = \(5.7 \times 10^{-3}\), then falls at higher rates — consistent with Part 1’s observation that Hyperball destabilizes at high LR.
  • MuonH collapses at high LR. Unique TF profiles drop from 1,280 to 571 between lr = \(5.7 \times 10^{-3}\) and \(2.3 \times 10^{-2}\), and grammar deteriorates sharply. This is the biological consequence of the spectral squeeze predicted in Part 1.
  • AdamW is flat. Its biological metrics vary least across the LR range. It never achieves the peak performance of the other optimizers, but it never catastrophically fails either.

The heatmaps reveal that the best optimizer depends on which biological dimension you care about — and that learning rate sensitivity varies by optimizer. AdamH is concentrated: its peak performance is narrow but high. MuonW is expansive: it achieves competitive results across a wider LR range for motif metrics, with the absolute best results at the extremes.

Threshold Analysis: Efficiency Across the Sweep

In Part 1, we measured FLOP efficiency using time-to-target: how many FLOPs to reach a perplexity of 2.51. Here we apply the same logic to biology. Instead of asking “which optimizer reaches the best score,” we ask: how many learning rates achieve a given biological quality threshold?

An optimizer that clears a threshold at 4 out of 5 learning rates is more efficient than one that only clears it at 1 — you spend fewer runs on hyperparameter search.

The threshold analysis makes the efficiency story concrete:

  • For grammar (Mantel r > 0.60): AdamH clears this at 4/5 LRs. MuonW at 2/5. AdamW at 2/5. MuonH at 1/5. If you need reliable grammar preservation, AdamH gets you there with far less tuning.
  • For motif fidelity (rate correlation > 0.70): MuonW clears this at 3/5 LRs. AdamH at 2/5. MuonH at 1/5. AdamW at 0/5. MuonW is the most efficient path to high motif recovery.
  • For diversity (>1400 profiles): MuonW and AdamH each clear this at 2/5 LRs. AdamW at 2/5. Only MuonW reaches >1500.

The peak-vs-mean comparison captures the key trade-off: MuonW has the highest peak on motif correlation and diversity, but large variance. AdamH has the highest peak on grammar, with moderate variance. AdamW has lower peaks but the tightest distribution — it wastes the fewest FLOPs on suboptimal runs.

TF Grammar: Learned Early, Sensitive to LR

The convergence analysis above showed that all optimizers learn TF co-occurrence grammar to similar levels (Mantel r ≈ 0.75–0.85) within the first few epochs. Grammar is not the bottleneck — abundance is. However, the LR sweep reveals that grammar quality at the final checkpoint is sensitive to learning rate, and here AdamH has an advantage: it achieves the highest grammar at its best LR and maintains good grammar across the widest range of learning rates.

The interaction between gradient processing and weight normalization is asymmetric:

Why Muon learns more motifs: the spectral argument

Recall from Part 1 that Muon’s Newton-Schulz whitening pushes all singular values of the update \(\tilde{u}_t = UV^\top\) toward 1, producing an approximately orthogonal update matrix. This has a direct consequence for motif learning.

Consider the weight matrix \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) with SVD \(W = U_W \Sigma_W V_W^\top\). The columns of \(V_W\) define the “input directions” the model attends to. When Muon updates \(W\), it distributes gradient energy across all singular directions equally. In the context of DNA, this means the model explores the full space of local sequence patterns simultaneously — no motif direction is starved of gradient signal. The result: more TFs recovered, higher stable rank, broader motif coverage.

Adam, by contrast, scales each coordinate by \(1/\sqrt{v_k + \varepsilon}\), where \(v_k\) is the running second moment for coordinate \(k\). Because DNA’s 4-token vocabulary is near-uniform, these second moments are approximately equal across coordinates (as shown in Part 1). Adam’s updates therefore behave like scaled SGD — they do not actively equalize singular directions the way Muon does, resulting in lower effective rank and fewer motifs explored.

Why Adam preserves grammar: the anisotropy argument

TF co-occurrence grammar is fundamentally anisotropic. Some TF pairs (e.g., GATA1–TAL1 in erythroid enhancers) co-occur at 20× the rate of others. Learning this structure requires the model to maintain different effective learning rates for different feature interactions — the “GATA1 × TAL1” direction in weight space should be updated differently than the “GATA1 × HNF4A” direction.

Adam’s per-coordinate scaling naturally preserves this heterogeneity. Even though the individual token frequencies are uniform, the gradient second moments for co-occurrence features are not — they reflect the non-uniform distribution of TF pair frequencies in the training data. Adam’s \(1/\sqrt{v_k}\) denominator adapts to these varying magnitudes, allowing high-frequency co-occurrence patterns to be reinforced while low-frequency patterns are explored more aggressively.

Muon’s whitening explicitly destroys this heterogeneity. By mapping all singular values to approximately 1, it treats the “GATA1 × TAL1” direction and the “GATA1 × HNF4A” direction identically. This is optimal for discovering which TFs exist (since all directions are explored equally), but suboptimal for encoding the relational structure between them.

Formal sketch: update geometry and co-occurrence structure

Let \(C \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times T}\) be the TF co-occurrence matrix from the training data, where \(C_{ij}\) is the frequency of TFs \(i\) and \(j\) appearing in the same regulatory element. The model learns a representation that implicitly encodes \(C\) through the weight matrices.

For Adam, the effective update for weights encoding co-occurrence feature \((i,j)\) scales as:

\[ \Delta w_{ij}^{\text{Adam}} \propto \frac{g_{ij}}{\sqrt{v_{ij} + \varepsilon}} \]

where \(v_{ij} \propto C_{ij} \cdot \mathbb{E}[\|\bar{g}_{ij}\|^2]\). For frequent co-occurrences (large \(C_{ij}\)), the denominator is large, providing a natural regularization that prevents overfitting to common patterns while giving rare patterns larger effective step sizes.

For Muon, the SVD whitening maps all directions to unit scale:

\[ \Delta W^{\text{Muon}} = -\eta \cdot U_g V_g^\top \]

where \(U_g \Sigma_g V_g^\top = \text{SVD}(g_t)\). The singular values \(\Sigma_g\) — which carry information about which co-occurrence directions have the strongest gradient signal — are discarded. This makes the update magnitude-blind: all directions receive equal-norm updates regardless of their prevalence in the data.

The consequence is that Adam’s updates preserve an implicit “frequency prior” on co-occurrence structure, while Muon’s updates impose a uniform prior. For motif discovery (first-order statistics), the uniform prior is optimal. For co-occurrence grammar (second-order statistics), the frequency prior better matches the data distribution.

Why this generalizes

This argument depends on optimizer mechanics, not on our specific architecture. Any model that learns from sequential DNA data faces the same tension:

  • First-order features (which TFs are present) are close to uniformly discoverable — each motif is a local 6–20bp pattern that can be learned independently. Muon’s uniform exploration advantage applies whenever the feature space is approximately isotropic at the individual feature level.
  • Second-order features (which TFs co-occur) are heterogeneous — their frequencies follow a heavy-tailed distribution set by the biology of gene regulation. Adam’s frequency-adaptive scaling is advantageous whenever the interaction structure is anisotropic.

This mirrors findings in NLP, where spectral methods excel at vocabulary coverage but adaptive methods better capture collocations and long-range dependencies3The analogy: motif = word, co-occurrence grammar = collocation. Muon learns more words; Adam learns better sentence structure. The same tension exists in language modeling, where Muon excels at per-token perplexity but Adam-family optimizers often produce more coherent generation.. The specific architecture (Axis, 85M parameters, transformer-based) determines the magnitude of the effect, but the qualitative direction — whitening helps first-order features, adaptive scaling helps second-order features — should hold across genomic foundation models.

Spectral whitening ≠ grammar

This updates Part 1’s narrative. Muon produces spectrally white weights (entropy 0.955 vs 0.743 for AdamW) — that is factually correct. But the implicit assumption that whitening captures the combinatorial grammar of the genome is not supported. The model that learns the most words doesn’t necessarily learn the best sentences.

The Diversity Paradox

Generated sequences have higher k-mer diversity than the reference (87–95% of 10-mers vs 77%). Yet at the functional level, they are less diverse (1,200–1,700 unique TF profiles vs 2,108). This is superficial diversity without functional variety — real regulatory elements are structured, and functional motifs create repetitive k-mer patterns that lower diversity. MuonW comes closest to closing the functional diversity gap (80% of reference).

What the Weights Tell Us: CKA

We computed Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA)2Linear CKA measures representation similarity. Range [0, 1], with 1 = identical representations up to linear transform. between all four best-LR models using 500 reference sequences.

Gradient processing determines what the model learns internally. Weight normalization modulates how that representation maps to biological output. This explains the asymmetric interaction: Muon’s representation is “locked in” by the whitening — Hyperball can’t reshape it. Adam’s representation is more malleable, and Hyperball reshapes it in a way that preserves relational structure.

The MuonH Cautionary Tale

MuonH at lr = \(2.27 \times 10^{-2}\) produces only 571 unique TF profiles (27% of reference) and condition numbers reaching \(2.64 \times 10^{10}\). The Malinois MPRA model predicts uniformly high, non-specific transcriptional activity — sequences that score as generically “active” without encoding any cell-type-specific program.

This is the biological consequence of the spectral squeeze described in Part 1. The effective learning rate for the up_proj layer reaches ~0.052 on the unit hypersphere, causing rank collapse and repetitive, non-specific generation.

Beyond Our Model

These results come from a single architecture (Axis, ~85M parameters) trained on ENCODE cis-regulatory elements. The natural question is whether the findings are specific to our setup or reflect something general about how optimizers interact with biological sequence structure. We argue the latter, for three reasons.

1. The convergence asymmetry is a property of the data, not the model

The most robust finding from our per-checkpoint analysis is that grammar converges before abundance — and this holds for all four optimizers. This is not an optimizer property; it is an information-theoretic property of regulatory DNA.

Co-occurrence grammar is low-dimensional and redundant. The number of biologically meaningful TF pairs is small (on the order of hundreds), and each pair appears in thousands of training sequences. A model needs relatively few gradient updates to capture which TFs tend to co-occur — the signal is strong and repeated. Motif abundance, by contrast, is high-dimensional: the model must learn the correct relative frequency of each TF across cell types and regulatory element classes, requiring it to calibrate a much larger set of parameters against sparser signal. Any architecture learning from regulatory DNA — whether transformer, CNN, or diffusion-based — faces this same asymmetry.

2. The optimizer advantage is mechanistic, not architectural

Muon’s advantage on motif abundance follows directly from its update rule. Newton-Schulz whitening maps the gradient SVD to \(\tilde{u}_t = UV^\top\), discarding the singular values \(\Sigma\). This produces an update that distributes energy equally across all directions in weight space. For DNA — where the 4-token vocabulary produces a near-isotropic gradient landscape at the individual position level — this means the model explores the full motif feature space simultaneously. No motif direction is starved of gradient signal.

Adam’s per-coordinate \(1/\sqrt{v_k}\) scaling, by contrast, adapts to the local curvature of each parameter. While this provides negligible benefit for individual nucleotide prediction (as Part 1 showed), it preserves the heterogeneous magnitude structure of gradient components that encode feature interactions. Co-occurrence features with high training frequency accumulate larger \(v_k\), receiving naturally regularized updates; rare co-occurrences receive larger effective step sizes. This implicit frequency prior matches the heavy-tailed distribution of TF pair frequencies in real regulatory DNA.

These properties are intrinsic to the optimizer update rules. They do not depend on the number of layers, the hidden dimension, or the specific attention mechanism. Any model using Muon will benefit from isotropic exploration; any model using Adam will preserve gradient anisotropies. The magnitude of the effect will vary with architecture, but the direction — whitening helps first-order features, adaptive scaling helps second-order features — follows from the mathematics.

3. The pattern has a direct analog in NLP

The tension between first-order and second-order learning is not unique to DNA. In natural language, Muon has been shown to excel at per-token perplexity — the language analog of motif abundance4The analogy: individual TF motifs are to DNA what individual words are to language. Co-occurrence grammar (GATA1 with TAL1) maps to collocations and idioms (“strong tea” but not “powerful tea”). Muon learns more words; Adam learns better sentences.. Adam-family optimizers often produce more coherent long-range structure, which maps to our finding that Adam better preserves combinatorial grammar. The same optimizer tension appears in both domains because the underlying mathematical structure — isotropic first-order statistics vs anisotropic second-order interactions — is shared.

This is not to say the effect will be identical everywhere. Architecture choices (depth, attention pattern, normalization scheme) modulate how strongly the optimizer signal propagates to biological output. But the qualitative prediction is clear: for any genomic foundation model, spectral whitening should accelerate motif coverage while adaptive scaling should better preserve relational structure. Our results provide the first direct evidence for this prediction in the DNA domain.

Open questions

Does grammar quality translate to functional activity? Our grammar metrics measure TF co-occurrence from motif scanning. The ultimate test is whether sequences with higher Mantel r show higher activity in reporter assays. We have Malinois predictions but not wet-lab validation.

Can a hybrid schedule capture both advantages? MuonW excels at abundance; AdamH excels at grammar robustness across LRs. A schedule that uses Muon for early-stage motif exploration and switches to Adam for late-stage grammar refinement might capture both — the convergence curves suggest this is plausible, since grammar is already high when abundance is still climbing.

How does this interact with scale? Part 1 showed that the MuonW advantage widens at larger model widths. Whether the biological advantages scale similarly remains untested.

Summary

Part 1 showed that Muon reaches target perplexity with 37% fewer FLOPs. Part 2 shows that this efficiency advantage extends to biology: MuonW reaches peak motif abundance by epoch 8, roughly 2× faster than Adam variants.

But the story is richer than “Muon wins.” Our per-checkpoint analysis reveals that biological quality is not monolithic. Grammar — the combinatorial logic of TF co-occurrence — converges early and is largely optimizer-insensitive. Abundance — how many binding sites the model recovers — is where the optimizers diverge, and where Muon’s whitening provides its largest advantage.

The mechanistic explanation follows from the update rules themselves. Muon’s spectral whitening imposes a uniform prior over weight-space directions, enabling broad exploration of the motif landscape. Adam’s adaptive scaling preserves the natural anisotropy of the co-occurrence feature space. These are not quirks of our architecture — they are mathematical properties of the optimizers that should hold across genomic foundation models wherever the same tension between first-order coverage and second-order relational structure exists.

For the field, the implication is that optimizer choice in genomic AI is not merely an engineering decision about training speed. It is a modeling decision that shapes which aspects of biological structure the model learns fastest and best. As genomic foundation models scale to longer contexts, more cell types, and more complex regulatory logic, understanding this interaction becomes essential — not just for efficiency, but for biological fidelity.
