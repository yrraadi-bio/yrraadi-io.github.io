Does Muon improve regulatory DNA learning? Part 2.

March 2026 · Akshat Gupta, Viraj Doshi

Highlights AdamW = Adam + Weight Decay
MuonW = Muon + Weight Decay
AdamH = Adam + Hyperball
MuonH = Muon + Hyperball

  • All four optimizers converge to nearly identical training loss (~1.20), yet produce substantially different biological quality in generated sequences — training loss does not predict biological performance.
  • MuonW excels at motif abundance and diversity: highest TF hit rate correlation (0.710 avg) and most unique regulatory programs (80% of reference).
  • AdamH excels at TF grammar preservation: best co-occurrence structure (Mantel r = 0.775), 22% better than the next optimizer. Hyperball helps Adam but hurts Muon — the interaction is asymmetric.
  • Spectral entropy (weight “whiteness”) does not correlate with grammar quality (ρ = 0.205, p = 0.387). The model that learns the most words doesn’t necessarily learn the best sentences.
  • CKA analysis shows gradient processing (Adam vs Muon) determines what the model learns internally, while weight normalization modulates how that maps to biological output.

In Part 1, we showed that MuonW reaches target validation perplexity with roughly 37% fewer FLOPs than AdamH. We dissected the spectral mechanics: Muon’s Newton-Schulz whitening produces weight matrices with near-maximal spectral entropy, distributing learned information across all singular value directions rather than concentrating it in a few dominant modes.

But perplexity measures how well a model predicts the next nucleotide. It does not measure whether the model has learned the regulatory grammar that makes enhancers function in cells.

An enhancer is not a bag of nucleotides. It is a precisely orchestrated arrangement of transcription factor binding sites — specific motifs appearing in specific combinations, at specific spacings, on specific strands. A model that achieves low loss by memorizing nucleotide statistics could still generate sequences where GATA1 and TAL1 binding sites never co-occur, or where promoter-specific motifs appear in distal enhancers. These sequences would look like DNA. They would not work like DNA.

This post answers the question Part 1 left open: does Muon’s training efficiency advantage translate to better biological quality in generated regulatory sequences?

The short answer is: it depends on which dimension of biology you measure.

The Loss–Biology Gap

All four optimizer configurations — AdamW, AdamH, MuonW, MuonH — converge to nearly identical training loss (~1.20 per nucleotide) after 20 epochs. Yet when we evaluate the generated sequences against a comprehensive biological quality suite, they produce substantially different results.

Why should identical loss produce different biology? Consider what the cross-entropy loss actually optimizes. It averages over all positions equally, but regulatory function concentrates in the ~10–20bp motif cores that occupy <10% of the sequence. A model that predicts flanking nucleotides perfectly but misses the binding site grammar can still achieve low per-position loss. The gap between “good at predicting nucleotides” and “good at generating functional regulatory elements” is the central finding of this post.

The Evaluation Suite

Before presenting results, we need to establish what “biological quality” means for generated regulatory DNA. We developed a six-axis evaluation framework, each axis targeting a distinct aspect of regulatory biology:

TF binding grammar measures whether generated sequences preserve the combinatorial logic of transcription factor co-occurrence. Real erythroid enhancers don’t just contain GATA1 binding sites — they contain GATA1 together with TAL1 and KLF1, at specific relative spacings. We quantify this using the Frobenius distance between reference and generated co-occurrence matrices, and test structural similarity with a proper Mantel permutation test (10,000 label permutations).

Motif abundance and fidelity measures how many TF binding sites are preserved and whether their relative frequencies match the reference. We scan all 16,249 sequences per condition against 22 JASPAR vertebrate motif matrices, computing per-TF hit rates, aggregate cosine similarity, and per-sequence Jensen-Shannon divergence.

Predicted transcriptional activity uses the Malinois MPRA model1Malinois is trained on real Massively Parallel Reporter Assay data and predicts log2 fold-change of transcriptional activity from sequence alone. It is the closest computational proxy to wet-lab functional validation. to predict whether generated sequences would drive gene expression in K562 (erythroid), HepG2 (hepatocyte), and SK-N-SH (neural) cell lines.

Cell-type specificity tests whether sequences generated with a K562 prompt contain K562-appropriate TFs (GATA1, TAL1, KLF1) and not hepatocyte TFs (HNF4A, FOXA). We use Fisher’s exact tests with Holm-Bonferroni correction.

Regulatory element type fidelity tests whether generated promoters look like promoters (high CpG O/E, SP1 enrichment) and enhancers look like enhancers (intermediate GC, cell-type-specific TFs).

Sequence diversity measures mode collapse: unique TF binding profiles, pairwise Jaccard similarity, and k-mer diversity at k=10.

We evaluate all four optimizers across five learning rates (\(9 \times 10^{-5}\) to \(2.27 \times 10^{-2}\)), yielding 20 trained models plus the reference dataset. All generations use temperature 0.5, which we found universally improves all motif metrics regardless of optimizer.

Where Muon Shines: Motif Abundance

The dimension where Muon most clearly outperforms Adam is raw motif recovery and diversity.

Across all 20 optimizer × learning rate configurations, the Muon family consistently produces sequences with more transcription factor binding sites. MuonW achieves the highest average TF hit rate correlation with reference (0.710 ± 0.077) compared to AdamW (0.608 ± 0.020). At its best learning rate (\(2.27 \times 10^{-2}\)), MuonW recovers 67.2% of all reference motif hits — the highest of any configuration.

MuonW also leads on sequence diversity: 1,689 unique TF binding profiles compared to 1,510 (AdamH), 1,418 (AdamW), and 1,285 (MuonH). This is 80% of the reference diversity (2,108 unique profiles) — the closest any optimizer achieves.

Why does Muon learn more motifs?

Muon’s Newton-Schulz whitening pushes all singular values of the gradient update toward \([0.7, 1.3]\), distributing the update across the full rank of the weight matrix. In DNA’s near-uniform 4-token space, this means the model explores the motif landscape broadly rather than fixating on the most statistically dominant patterns. The result: more TFs recovered, more diverse combinations, closer to the reference distribution. This is consistent with MuonW’s higher stable rank (449 vs 63 for AdamW) — more “active dimensions” in the weight matrices correlate with more diverse motif recovery.

Where Adam Surprises: TF Grammar

Here is where the story gets interesting. Despite Muon’s clear advantage in motif abundance, AdamH preserves the combinatorial grammar of transcription factor co-occurrence significantly better than any Muon variant.

AdamH at its best learning rate (\(5.69 \times 10^{-3}\)) achieves a Mantel correlation of 0.775 with the reference co-occurrence matrix — 22% higher than the next-best optimizer (MuonW at 0.626). Its Frobenius distance to the reference matrix (0.184) is the lowest, and its spacing JSD (0.244) is also best. This is not a cherry-picked result. Averaged across all five learning rates, AdamH maintains the highest grammar score (0.673) compared to MuonW (0.575), AdamW (0.548), and MuonH (0.459).

The pattern becomes even more striking when we examine the 2×2 factorial structure of our optimizer design:

Hyperball normalization improves Adam’s grammar by 23% (Mantel r: 0.548 → 0.673) but worsens Muon’s grammar by 20% (0.575 → 0.459). The interaction is asymmetric. Neither gradient processing alone nor weight normalization alone explains grammar quality — the specific combination matters.

Why might Adam preserve grammar better?

TF grammar requires the model to encode relationships between features, not just individual features. The co-occurrence matrix encodes which TF pairs tend to appear in the same regulatory element — GATA1 and TAL1 co-occur frequently in erythroid enhancers; GATA1 and HNF4A rarely co-occur anywhere.

In Part 1, we argued that Adam’s per-coordinate scaling provides “negligible benefit” in DNA’s near-uniform token space for individual nucleotide prediction. But the grammar of TF co-occurrence is not near-uniform — some pairs co-occur at 20× the rate of others. Adam’s coordinate-wise adaptation may be capturing exactly this heterogeneity in the feature interaction landscape. Muon’s whitening, by contrast, explicitly equalizes singular values — treating all directions in weight space as equally important. This is optimal for exploring the full motif vocabulary but may flatten the anisotropies that encode combinatorial relationships.

Spectral whitening ≠ grammar

This brings us to a key finding that directly updates Part 1’s narrative. We measured the Spearman correlation between spectral entropy and grammar quality across all 20 checkpoints: ρ = 0.205, p = 0.387. There is no significant relationship.

Part 1’s claim that Muon produces spectrally white weights is factually correct — we measured it directly (entropy 0.955 vs 0.743 for AdamW). But the implicit assumption that spectral whitening captures “the complex, combinatorial grammar of the genome” is not supported by the grammar metrics. Having more whitened weights correlates with better motif abundance, but not with better grammar.

The model that learns the most words doesn’t necessarily learn the best sentences.

The Diversity Paradox

Generated sequences have higher k-mer diversity than the reference dataset. At k=10, generated sequences cover 87–95% of possible 10-mers, compared to 77% for real regulatory elements. Yet at the functional level — unique TF binding profiles — generated sequences are substantially less diverse (1,200–1,700 unique profiles vs 2,108).

This is the diversity paradox: superficial diversity without functional variety.

Real regulatory elements are highly structured. Functional motifs create repetitive k-mer patterns — the GATA1 consensus WGATAR appears thousands of times across erythroid enhancers, lowering k-mer diversity. Generated sequences produce more uniformly distributed nucleotide patterns (higher k-mer entropy) while converging on fewer distinct regulatory programs. This finding holds across all optimizers, though MuonW comes closest to closing the gap on functional diversity (80% of reference).

What the Weights Tell Us: CKA and the Gradient Hypothesis

Part 1 characterized what the weight matrices look like after training. We can now ask: do the models learn the same internal representations, and does that explain their biological differences?

We computed Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA)2Linear CKA (Kornblith et al., ICML 2019): \(\text{CKA}(X,Y) = \|Y^\top X\|_F^2 / (\|X^\top X\|_F \cdot \|Y^\top Y\|_F)\), where \(X\) and \(Y\) are centered activation matrices. Range [0, 1], with 1 = identical representations up to linear transform. between all four best-LR models, using 500 reference sequences as input.

The pattern is unambiguous:

  • Muon pair (MuonW ↔ MuonH): CKA = 0.941 — nearly identical representations
  • Adam pair (AdamW ↔ AdamH): CKA = 0.724 — moderately similar
  • Cross-gradient pairs: CKA = 0.40–0.49 — substantially different

Gradient processing determines what the model learns. Newton-Schulz whitening produces nearly identical internal representations regardless of weight normalization. Per-coordinate Adam is more sensitive to whether you apply weight decay or Hyperball.

This explains a key puzzle: why does Hyperball help Adam’s grammar but hurt Muon’s? Because Muon’s representation is already “locked in” by the whitening — adding Hyperball doesn’t reshape the representation, it just constrains how that fixed representation maps to outputs. For Adam, the representation is more malleable, and Hyperball’s constraint reshapes it in a way that preserves relational structure.

The MuonH Cautionary Tale

In Part 1, we identified a theoretical failure mode: combining Newton-Schulz whitening with Hyperball creates a “spectral squeeze” where both the gradient and the weight normalization destroy magnitude information, potentially leading to rank collapse. The biological data confirms this prediction.

MuonH at lr = \(2.27 \times 10^{-2}\) produces only 571 unique TF profiles — 27% of the reference. Its weight matrices have condition numbers reaching \(2.64 \times 10^{10}\) (compared to 3,440 at the healthy lr = \(9 \times 10^{-5}\)). The Malinois MPRA model predicts uniformly high, non-specific transcriptional activity: these are sequences that score as generically “active” without encoding any cell-type-specific regulatory program.

The mechanism is exactly what Part 1 predicted. For the up_proj layer (10922 × 2048), the effective learning rate is \(\eta \times \sqrt{\text{fan\_in}/\text{fan\_out}} = 0.0227 \times \sqrt{10922/2048} \approx 0.052\) — an enormous step on a unit-norm hypersphere. The weight matrices become extremely ill-conditioned, and the model collapses to generating repetitive, non-specific sequences.

Temperature: The Elephant in the Room

One finding we must be transparent about: sampling temperature has a larger effect on biological quality than optimizer choice. Dropping from temperature 1.0 to 0.5 universally improves all motif metrics across all optimizers.

However, the effect is not uniform. Adam variants benefit disproportionately from lower temperature (AdamW: +79%, AdamH: +57%) compared to Muon variants (MuonH: +32%, MuonW: +26%). This connects back to Part 1’s “activation hacking” observation: MuonW’s unbounded weight norm growth effectively reduces the softmax temperature during training, sharpening predictions. Lower explicit temperature at generation time does the same thing. Some of MuonW’s biological advantage may therefore reflect implicit temperature reduction rather than genuinely superior representation learning.

What We Don’t Know Yet

Several questions remain open:

Does grammar translate to function? Our grammar metrics measure TF co-occurrence from motif scanning. The ultimate test is whether AdamH-generated sequences show higher activity in reporter assays than MuonW-generated sequences. We have Malinois predictions (a model of biology) but not biology itself.

Can we get the best of both? MuonW excels at abundance; AdamH excels at grammar. A hybrid training schedule — Muon for early exploration, Adam for late-stage grammar refinement — might capture both advantages. We have not tested this.

Is the activation hacking real? MuonW’s growing weight norms blur the line between genuine learning and implicit temperature reduction. A controlled experiment with fixed output-head norms would isolate this effect.

Does this generalize? Our results are for a single architecture (Axis, ~85M parameters) trained on a specific regulatory DNA dataset. The optimizer × biology interaction may differ for other architectures or genomic contexts.

Summary

Part 1 showed that Muon is faster. Part 2 shows that faster does not automatically mean biologically better — but it does mean biologically different.

Muon’s spectral whitening produces models that learn more of the motif vocabulary and generate more diverse regulatory sequences. Adam’s per-coordinate scaling, when combined with Hyperball normalization, preserves the combinatorial grammar of transcription factor co-occurrence — the higher-order structure that determines whether an enhancer actually functions.

The most important finding is that spectral entropy — the “whiteness” that Muon’s proponents highlight as a mechanistic advantage — does not correlate with grammar quality. A model can have perfectly distributed singular values and still fail to encode which TFs belong together. The axes of biological quality are not aligned with the axes of weight matrix geometry.

For practitioners, the choice is clear: match the optimizer to the biological objective. For researchers, the gradient × weight-normalization interaction — and its asymmetric effect on different biological dimensions — suggests that optimizer design for genomic foundation models deserves more attention than it currently receives.
