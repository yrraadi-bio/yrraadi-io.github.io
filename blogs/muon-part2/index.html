Does Muon improve regulatory DNA learning? Part 2.

March 2026 · Akshat Singh, Viraj Doshi

Highlights AdamW = Adam + Weight Decay
MuonW = Muon + Weight Decay
AdamH = Adam + Hyperball
MuonH = Muon + Hyperball

  • We evaluate generated sequences at every training epoch across 5 biological tests. For each test, we compare the best Muon configuration against the best Adam configuration on two axes: how fast each reaches a quality threshold, and how good the final result is.
  • Motif abundance (how many TF binding sites the model recovers, measured by Pearson r of per-TF hit rates): MuonW converges to r = 0.81 by epoch 8, while AdamH takes until epoch 14 to reach r = 0.67. MuonW is roughly 2× faster to biological quality on this axis.
  • TF grammar (whether the correct TFs co-occur in the same sequences, measured by Mantel correlation of co-occurrence matrices): all optimizers reach comparable quality (Mantel r ≈ 0.75–0.85) within the first few epochs. Grammar is not the bottleneck.
  • Muon’s spectral whitening distributes gradient signal uniformly, enabling broad motif exploration. Adam’s per-coordinate scaling preserves the heterogeneous frequency structure of TF co-occurrence. These are optimizer-level properties that generalize beyond our architecture.

In Part 1, we showed that MuonW reaches target validation perplexity with roughly 37% fewer FLOPs than AdamH. We ended with an open question: “does this translate to improvements on tasks that matter for biology, such as transcription factor motif recovery, and sequence generation?”

This post answers that question with a structured biological evaluation. We take the best Muon and best Adam configurations, evaluate them at every training epoch, and measure five dimensions of biological quality. For each test, we ask: which optimizer converges faster, and which produces better final sequences?

Can Similar Validation Perplexity Produce Different Biology?

All four optimizers converge to similar validation perplexity (~1.20 CE loss) by epoch 20. Yet as we show below, the sequences they generate have substantially different biological properties.

Why? Optimization is program search: two regions of parameter space can produce similar cross-entropy losses on the same distribution while prioritizing different features. The CE loss averages over all positions equally, but regulatory function concentrates in ~10–20bp motif cores that occupy <10% of the sequence. A model can achieve low loss by predicting flanking nucleotides well while encoding very different amounts of motif structure.

Evaluation Suite

We evaluate five biological dimensions. Each test is designed to answer a specific question about the quality of generated regulatory DNA:

Test 1 — Motif Abundance: Does the model recover the right transcription factor binding sites at the right frequencies? We scan all 16,249 generated sequences against 22 JASPAR TF motif matrices using MOODS, count hits per TF, and compute the Pearson r between the generated and reference per-TF hit rate vectors. High r means the model produces the correct relative abundance of each TF. This is a first-order statistic — it measures individual motif presence, not relationships between motifs.

Test 2 — TF Grammar: Do the correct TFs appear together in the same sequences? Real erythroid enhancers contain GATA1 with TAL1 and KLF1 — not GATA1 with hepatocyte factors like HNF4A. For each sequence, we record which TFs are present, then build a co-occurrence matrix counting how often each TF pair appears in the same sequence. We compare the generated and reference co-occurrence matrices using the Mantel test (Pearson r of the upper-triangle vectors, with significance assessed by 10,000 label permutations). This is a second-order statistic — it measures pairwise relationships.

Test 3 — Sequence Diversity: Does the model collapse to generating the same few regulatory programs, or does it produce varied sequences? We compute the number of unique TF binding profiles — distinct combinations of which TFs are present in each sequence. The reference dataset contains 2,108 unique profiles. Fewer unique profiles = more mode collapse. We also measure k-mer diversity (fraction of possible 10-mers observed) as a nucleotide-level diversity check.

Test 4 — Representation Similarity (CKA): Do different optimizers learn the same internal representations? We compute Centered Kernel Alignment (Linear CKA) between models, using 500 reference sequences as input. CKA = 1 means identical representations up to linear transform; CKA ≈ 0 means unrelated. This tells us whether the optimizer shapes what the model learns internally, not just the output quality.

Test 5 — Spectral Stability: Does the model maintain healthy weight matrix structure throughout training? We track spectral entropy (how uniformly variance is distributed across singular values), stable rank (effective dimensionality), and condition number (ratio of largest to smallest singular value) across training. Collapse to low entropy / high condition number indicates representational degeneration.

For Tests 1–3, we evaluate at every training epoch (epochs 1–19) at the best learning rate per optimizer, giving us biological convergence curves — the direct analog of Part 1’s perplexity progression plots. We also sweep all 20 configurations (4 optimizers × 5 learning rates) at the final checkpoint for a broader view.

Test 1: Motif Abundance

Convergence: MuonW vs AdamH

MuonW converges to peak motif abundance roughly 2× faster than AdamH. MuonW’s Pearson r rises steeply from 0.37 at epoch 2 to 0.81 at epoch 8. AdamH improves slowly, reaching its peak (~0.67) only at epoch 14. AdamW hovers between 0.29 and 0.47 across the entire training run. In Part 1, we showed MuonW reaches target perplexity with 37% fewer FLOPs. Here the biological advantage is even larger.

LR sweep context

Across the full LR sweep, MuonW achieves the highest peak motif abundance (r = 0.824) and maintains high abundance across a wider LR range than any other optimizer.

Test 2: TF Grammar

Convergence: all optimizers reach similar grammar early

The key finding: grammar is not the bottleneck. All four optimizers reach Mantel r ≈ 0.75–0.85 within the first few epochs. The co-occurrence structure of TF binding — which TFs tend to appear together — is apparently one of the first things the model captures, regardless of gradient processing.

LR sweep context

At the final checkpoint, grammar quality varies with learning rate, and AdamH achieves the best score at its optimal LR. The Hyperball × gradient interaction is asymmetric: Hyperball improves Adam’s grammar by 23% but worsens Muon’s by 20%.

Test 3: Sequence Diversity

Generated sequences show higher k-mer diversity (87–95% of possible 10-mers) than real regulatory elements (77%). Yet they produce fewer unique TF binding profiles (1,200–1,700 vs 2,108 in the reference). Real regulatory DNA is structured — functional motifs like the GATA1 consensus WGATAR create repetitive k-mer patterns that lower nucleotide diversity. Generated sequences are more random at the nucleotide level but converge on fewer distinct regulatory programs.

MuonW produces the most functionally diverse sequences (1,689 unique profiles, 80% of reference), consistent with its motif abundance advantage. AdamW produces the fewest (1,242). This is a direct consequence of motif coverage: more TFs recovered means more possible combinations.

Test 4: Representation Similarity

Gradient processing determines what the model learns internally. MuonW and MuonH — despite producing different biological output quality — encode nearly identical representations (CKA = 0.941). Changing the weight normalization (WD vs Hyperball) barely affects the internal representation for Muon, but meaningfully reshapes it for Adam (CKA = 0.724). This means the biological differences between MuonW and MuonH come from how the representation maps to outputs, not from what the representation encodes.

Test 5: Spectral Stability

Part 1 predicted that combining Newton-Schulz whitening with Hyperball creates a “spectral squeeze” that can collapse the weight spectrum. The biological data confirms this: MuonH at high learning rates produces only 571 unique TF profiles (27% of reference), with non-specific Malinois MPRA activity — sequences that score as generically “active” across all cell types without encoding any cell-type-specific program. The effective learning rate for the up_proj layer reaches ~0.052 on the unit hypersphere, far beyond the stable regime.

Why Muon and Adam Differ

Why Muon learns more motifs: the spectral argument

Muon’s Newton-Schulz whitening produces an approximately orthogonal update \(\tilde{u}_t = UV^\top\), distributing gradient energy equally across all singular directions. In DNA’s near-isotropic 4-token space, this means the model explores the full space of local sequence patterns simultaneously — no motif direction is starved of gradient signal. The result: higher stable rank (449 for MuonW vs 63 for AdamW), more TFs recovered, broader motif coverage.

Adam scales each coordinate by \(1/\sqrt{v_k + \varepsilon}\). Because DNA’s token frequencies are near-uniform, these second moments are approximately equal, and Adam’s updates behave like scaled SGD — they do not actively equalize singular directions the way Muon does.

Why Adam better preserves grammar at certain LRs: the anisotropy argument

TF co-occurrence is anisotropic: some TF pairs (GATA1–TAL1 in erythroid enhancers) co-occur at 20× the rate of others. While the per-checkpoint analysis shows all optimizers learn similar grammar early, Adam’s per-coordinate scaling helps preserve this structure across a wider range of learning rates. The gradient second moments for co-occurrence features reflect the non-uniform frequency distribution of TF pairs, providing an implicit frequency prior that matches the data.

Muon’s whitening discards the singular values of the gradient — the magnitudes that carry information about which co-occurrence directions have the strongest signal. This makes the update magnitude-blind: all directions receive equal-norm updates regardless of their prevalence in the data.

Formal sketch: update geometry and co-occurrence structure

For Adam, the effective update for weights encoding co-occurrence feature \((i,j)\) scales as:

\[ \Delta w_{ij}^{\text{Adam}} \propto \frac{g_{ij}}{\sqrt{v_{ij} + \varepsilon}} \]

where \(v_{ij} \propto C_{ij} \cdot \mathbb{E}[\|\bar{g}_{ij}\|^2]\). For frequent co-occurrences (large \(C_{ij}\)), the denominator is large, providing natural regularization. Rare patterns get larger effective step sizes.

For Muon, SVD whitening maps all directions to unit scale:

\[ \Delta W^{\text{Muon}} = -\eta \cdot U_g V_g^\top \]

The singular values \(\Sigma_g\) — which carry co-occurrence magnitude information — are discarded. Adam preserves an implicit frequency prior; Muon imposes a uniform prior. For first-order motif discovery, the uniform prior is optimal. For second-order co-occurrence grammar, the frequency prior better matches the data.

Beyond Our Model

These results come from a single architecture (Axis, ~85M parameters) trained on ENCODE cis-regulatory elements. We argue the findings generalize, for three reasons.

1. The convergence asymmetry is a property of the data

Grammar converges before abundance for all four optimizers. This is not an optimizer property — it is an information-theoretic property of regulatory DNA. Co-occurrence grammar is low-dimensional and redundant: hundreds of TF pairs, each appearing in thousands of sequences. Motif abundance calibration is high-dimensional: the model must learn the correct relative frequency of each TF across cell types and element classes. Any architecture learning from regulatory DNA faces this same asymmetry.

2. The optimizer advantage is mechanistic

Muon’s advantage follows from its update rule, not from our architecture. Newton-Schulz whitening distributes energy equally across all weight-space directions. For DNA’s near-isotropic token distribution, this means broad motif exploration. Adam’s \(1/\sqrt{v_k}\) scaling preserves gradient anisotropies that encode feature interaction frequencies. These properties are intrinsic to the optimizers — they do not depend on layer count, hidden dimension, or attention mechanism. The magnitude of the effect will vary with architecture, but the direction follows from the mathematics1The analogy to NLP: individual TF motifs map to words; co-occurrence grammar maps to collocations. Muon excels at per-token perplexity (vocabulary coverage); Adam-family optimizers often produce more coherent long-range structure. The same tension between isotropic first-order statistics and anisotropic second-order interactions appears in both domains..

3. Testable prediction

Our analysis makes a concrete prediction: for any genomic foundation model, spectral whitening should accelerate motif coverage while adaptive scaling should better preserve relational structure across learning rates. This can be tested on other architectures (Evo 2, Enformer, diffusion models) and other genomic contexts (whole-genome, epigenomic, multi-species). We provide the evaluation framework to do so.

Open questions

Does grammar quality translate to functional activity? Our grammar metrics measure TF co-occurrence from motif scanning. The ultimate test is wet-lab reporter assays.

Can a hybrid schedule capture both advantages? Muon for early-stage motif exploration, then Adam for late-stage refinement. The convergence curves suggest this is plausible — grammar is already high when abundance is still climbing.

How does this interact with scale? Part 1 showed that MuonW’s advantage widens at larger model widths. Whether the biological advantages scale similarly remains untested.

Conclusion

Spectral Stability MuonH collapses at high LR; MuonW and AdamW stable across all LRs

MuonW’s FLOP efficiency advantage from Part 1 extends directly to motif abundance: it reaches biological quality thresholds roughly 2× faster than Adam variants. Grammar — the combinatorial logic of TF co-occurrence — converges early for all optimizers and is not the bottleneck.

The mechanistic explanation is grounded in the optimizer update rules. Muon’s spectral whitening imposes a uniform prior over weight-space directions, enabling broad motif exploration. Adam’s adaptive scaling preserves co-occurrence anisotropies. These are not artifacts of our architecture — they are mathematical properties that should shape how any genomic foundation model learns regulatory structure.

Optimizer choice in genomic AI is not merely an engineering decision about training speed. It is a modeling decision that shapes which aspects of biological structure the model learns fastest and best.
