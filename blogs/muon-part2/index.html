Does Muon improve regulatory DNA learning? Part 2.

March 2026 · Akshat Singh, Viraj Doshi

Highlights AdamW = Adam + Weight Decay
MuonW = Muon + Weight Decay
AdamH = Adam + Hyperball
MuonH = Muon + Hyperball

  • Models that reach similar validation perplexity can still produce sequences with substantially different biological quality. We develop a biological evaluation suite to measure what loss cannot.
  • Muon’s training efficiency advantage from Part 1 extends to biology — but the story is more nuanced than “Muon wins again.” Different biological dimensions respond differently to optimizer choice.
  • We evaluate at every training epoch and across 20 optimizer × LR configurations. The results reveal that optimizer choice is not just a speed decision — it shapes which aspects of regulatory structure the model learns.

The Open Question from Part 1

In Part 1, we showed that MuonW reaches target validation perplexity with roughly 37% fewer FLOPs than AdamH. We dissected the spectral mechanics of why: Muon’s Newton-Schulz whitening distributes learned information across all singular value directions, while Adam concentrates it in fewer dominant modes.

We ended with an open question: “does this translate to improvements on tasks that matter for biology, such as transcription factor motif recovery, and sequence generation?”

This is not a trivial question. Validation perplexity measures how well a model predicts the next nucleotide. It does not measure whether the model has learned the regulatory grammar that makes enhancers function in cells. An enhancer is not a bag of nucleotides — it is a precisely orchestrated arrangement of transcription factor binding sites, appearing in specific combinations, at specific spacings. A model that achieves low perplexity by memorizing nucleotide statistics could still generate sequences where the wrong TFs co-occur, or where promoter-specific motifs appear in distal enhancers. To answer Part 1’s question, we need to evaluate the biology directly.

Why Similar Loss Can Produce Different Biology

All four optimizers converge to similar validation perplexity (~1.20 CE loss) by epoch 20. Yet as we show in the sections that follow, the sequences they generate have substantially different biological properties. Why?

Optimization is program search. Two regions of parameter space can produce similar cross-entropy on the same distribution while prioritizing learning different features. The CE loss averages over all positions equally, but regulatory function concentrates in the ~10–20bp motif cores that occupy <10% of the sequence. A model can achieve low average loss by predicting flanking nucleotides well while encoding very different amounts of motif structure. Think of it as a high-dimensional loss landscape with “hidden directions” that measure biological features — directions that the scalar loss does not distinguish.

This is the justification for everything that follows. To understand whether optimizer choice affects biological quality, we need evaluations that measure biology directly.

The Motif Vocabulary: Does the Model Recover the Right Parts?

Why this test matters

Before asking whether a model has learned regulatory logic, we first need to ask a more basic question: has it learned the regulatory parts list? Regulatory DNA function is driven by transcription factor binding sites — short motifs (6–20bp) that recruit specific proteins. If the model doesn’t produce these motifs at the correct frequencies, it cannot possibly produce functional regulatory elements.

To measure this, we scan all 16,249 generated sequences against 22 JASPAR TF motif matrices using MOODS, count hits per TF, and compute the Pearson r between the generated and reference per-TF hit rate vectors. High r means the model produces the correct relative abundance of each TF — it has learned which motifs are common (e.g., GATA1 in erythroid enhancers) and which are rare. This is a first-order statistic: it measures individual motif presence, not relationships between motifs.

What we observed

MuonW converges to peak motif abundance roughly 2× faster than AdamH. MuonW’s Pearson r rises steeply from 0.37 at epoch 2 to 0.81 at epoch 8. AdamH improves more slowly, reaching its peak (~0.67) at epoch 14. AdamW hovers between 0.29 and 0.47 across the entire training run. In Part 1, we showed MuonW reaches target perplexity with 37% fewer FLOPs. Here the biological advantage is even larger.

Why this makes sense

Biologically: motif abundance is a calibration problem — the model must learn the correct relative frequency of each TF across cell types and regulatory element classes. This requires exploring the full space of local sequence patterns, not just the most statistically dominant ones.

Mathematically: Muon’s Newton-Schulz whitening produces an approximately orthogonal update \(\tilde{u}_t = UV^\top\), distributing gradient energy equally across all singular directions. In DNA’s near-isotropic 4-token space, this means the model explores the full motif landscape simultaneously — no motif direction is starved of gradient signal. The result: higher stable rank (449 for MuonW vs 63 for AdamW), more TFs recovered, broader motif coverage. Adam’s per-coordinate \(1/\sqrt{v_k}\) scaling, because DNA’s token frequencies are near-uniform, provides approximately equal scaling across coordinates — it does not actively equalize singular directions the way Muon does.

The Combinatorial Grammar: Do the Right Parts Appear Together?

Why this test matters

Recovering individual motifs is necessary but not sufficient. Real erythroid enhancers contain GATA1 together with TAL1 and KLF1 — not GATA1 with hepatocyte factors like HNF4A. The combinatorial logic of which TFs co-occur in the same regulatory element is what makes the element functional in the right cell type. A model that recovers all the right motifs individually but pairs them incorrectly would produce sequences that look plausible but could not function.

To measure this, for each sequence we record which TFs are present, then build a co-occurrence matrix counting how often each TF pair appears in the same sequence. We compare the generated and reference co-occurrence matrices using the Mantel test: Pearson r of the upper-triangle vectors, with significance assessed by 10,000 label permutations. This is a second-order statistic — it measures pairwise relationships, not individual presence.

What we observed

Surprisingly, grammar is not the bottleneck. All four optimizers reach Mantel r ≈ 0.75–0.85 within the first few epochs. The co-occurrence structure of TF binding — which TFs tend to appear together — is one of the first things the model captures, regardless of gradient processing. The optimizer differences that dominate on motif abundance are largely absent on grammar convergence.

At the final checkpoint, grammar quality does vary with learning rate, and AdamH achieves the highest score at its optimal LR (Mantel r = 0.775). The Hyperball × gradient interaction is asymmetric: Hyperball improves Adam’s grammar by 23% but worsens Muon’s by 20%.

Why this makes sense

Biologically: co-occurrence grammar is low-dimensional and redundant. The number of biologically meaningful TF pairs is small (on the order of hundreds), and each pair appears in thousands of training sequences. A model needs relatively few gradient updates to capture which TFs tend to co-occur — the signal is strong and repeated. This explains why grammar converges fast for all optimizers.

Mathematically: TF co-occurrence frequencies follow a heavy-tailed distribution — some pairs (GATA1–TAL1) co-occur at 20× the rate of others. Adam’s per-coordinate \(1/\sqrt{v_k}\) scaling adapts to these varying magnitudes: gradient second moments for high-frequency co-occurrence features accumulate faster, providing implicit regularization, while rare co-occurrences receive larger effective step sizes. This “frequency prior” matches the data distribution. Muon’s whitening discards the singular values that carry this magnitude information, imposing a uniform prior over all co-occurrence directions. For grammar, the frequency prior is slightly better — but since grammar converges fast regardless, the practical impact is limited to LR sensitivity at the final checkpoint, not convergence speed.

Formal sketch: update geometry and co-occurrence structure

For Adam, the effective update for weights encoding co-occurrence feature \((i,j)\) scales as:

\[ \Delta w_{ij}^{\text{Adam}} \propto \frac{g_{ij}}{\sqrt{v_{ij} + \varepsilon}} \]

where \(v_{ij} \propto C_{ij} \cdot \mathbb{E}[\|\bar{g}_{ij}\|^2]\). For frequent co-occurrences (large \(C_{ij}\)), the denominator is large, providing natural regularization. Rare patterns get larger effective step sizes.

For Muon, SVD whitening maps all directions to unit scale:

\[ \Delta W^{\text{Muon}} = -\eta \cdot U_g V_g^\top \]

The singular values \(\Sigma_g\) — which carry co-occurrence magnitude information — are discarded. Adam preserves an implicit frequency prior; Muon imposes a uniform prior.

Functional Diversity: Does the Model Generate Varied Regulatory Programs?

Why this test matters

A model might recover the right motifs at the right frequencies in aggregate, yet still generate the same few regulatory programs over and over. This is mode collapse — and it matters because real regulatory genomes contain thousands of distinct enhancer programs, each with a unique combination of TF binding sites. A useful generative model must produce variety, not just correctness on average.

Raw nucleotide diversity (e.g., k-mer coverage) is not the right metric here. Biological diversity is diversity over functional regulatory programs — distinct combinations of which TFs are present in each sequence. We count the number of unique TF binding profiles in the generated set. The reference dataset contains 2,108 unique profiles; fewer unique profiles means more mode collapse.

What we observed

Generated sequences show higher k-mer diversity than real regulatory elements. Yet they produce fewer unique TF binding profiles. This is superficial diversity without functional variety. Real regulatory DNA is structured — functional motifs like the GATA1 consensus WGATAR create repetitive k-mer patterns that lower nucleotide diversity. Generated sequences are more random at the nucleotide level but converge on fewer distinct regulatory programs.

MuonW produces the most functionally diverse sequences (1,689 unique profiles, 80% of reference). AdamW produces the fewest (1,242).

Why this makes sense

Biologically: functional diversity is a direct consequence of motif coverage. More TFs recovered means more possible combinations of TFs per sequence. MuonW’s broader motif vocabulary naturally produces more distinct regulatory programs.

Mathematically: this is consistent with the spectral argument. Muon’s higher effective rank means the model’s weight matrices use more independent directions, enabling a larger space of distinct output programs. Lower effective rank (Adam, and especially collapsed MuonH) constrains the output to a smaller subspace.

Supporting Evidence: Representations and Spectral Stability

The three tests above measure biological output quality. Two additional analyses help explain why the optimizers differ, by looking at what happens inside the model.

Representation similarity: optimizer choice changes what the model encodes

If similar losses led to the same internal solution, optimizer choice would mostly be a speed story. To test this, we computed Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA)1Linear CKA measures representation similarity. CKA = 1 means identical representations up to linear transform; CKA ≈ 0 means unrelated. between all four best-LR models using 500 reference sequences.

The internal representations are not the same. Gradient processing — not weight normalization — is the dominant factor determining what the model encodes. MuonW and MuonH have nearly identical internal representations (CKA = 0.941) despite producing different output quality. This means the biological differences between MuonW and MuonH come from how the representation maps to outputs, not from what the representation encodes. For Adam, weight normalization has a larger effect on the representation itself (CKA = 0.724). Optimizer choice is not just a speed decision — it changes the solution the model finds.

Spectral stability: when optimizer geometry fails

Optimizer geometry can help, but it can also fail. Part 1 predicted that combining Newton-Schulz whitening with Hyperball creates a “spectral squeeze” — a pathology where both the gradient and the weight normalization destroy magnitude information, collapsing the weight spectrum.

The biological data confirms this prediction. MuonH at high learning rates produces only 571 unique TF profiles (27% of reference), with non-specific activity across all cell types. The effective learning rate for the up_proj layer reaches ~0.052 on the unit hypersphere, far beyond the stable regime. This is not just an abstract mathematical failure — it directly corresponds to biologically worse output. Spectral pathologies have measurable biological consequences.

Beyond Our Model

These results come from a single architecture (Axis, ~85M parameters) trained on ENCODE cis-regulatory elements. We argue the findings generalize, for three reasons.

1. The convergence asymmetry is a property of the data

Grammar converges before abundance for all four optimizers. This is not an optimizer property — it is an information-theoretic property of regulatory DNA. Co-occurrence grammar is low-dimensional and redundant: hundreds of TF pairs, each appearing in thousands of sequences. Motif abundance calibration is high-dimensional: the model must learn the correct relative frequency of each TF across cell types and element classes. Any architecture learning from regulatory DNA faces this same asymmetry.

2. The optimizer advantage is mechanistic

Muon’s advantage follows from its update rule, not from our architecture. Newton-Schulz whitening distributes energy equally across all weight-space directions. For DNA’s near-isotropic token distribution, this means broad motif exploration. Adam’s \(1/\sqrt{v_k}\) scaling preserves gradient anisotropies that encode feature interaction frequencies. These properties are intrinsic to the optimizers — they do not depend on layer count, hidden dimension, or attention mechanism2The analogy to NLP: individual TF motifs map to words; co-occurrence grammar maps to collocations. Muon excels at per-token perplexity (vocabulary coverage); Adam-family optimizers often produce more coherent long-range structure. The same tension between isotropic first-order statistics and anisotropic second-order interactions appears in both domains.. The magnitude of the effect will vary with architecture, but the direction follows from the mathematics.

3. A testable prediction

Our analysis makes a concrete prediction: for any genomic foundation model, spectral whitening should accelerate motif coverage while adaptive scaling should better preserve relational structure across learning rates. This can be tested on other architectures (Evo 2, Enformer, diffusion models) and other genomic contexts (whole-genome, epigenomic, multi-species). We provide the evaluation framework to do so.

Open questions

Does grammar quality translate to functional activity? Our grammar metrics measure TF co-occurrence from motif scanning. The ultimate test is wet-lab reporter assays.

Can a hybrid schedule capture both advantages? Muon for early-stage motif exploration, then Adam for late-stage refinement. The convergence curves suggest this is plausible — grammar is already high when abundance is still climbing.

How does this interact with scale? Part 1 showed that MuonW’s advantage widens at larger model widths. Whether the biological advantages scale similarly remains untested.

Part 1 showed that Muon is faster to target perplexity. Part 2 shows that this speed extends to motif abundance — MuonW reaches biological quality thresholds roughly 2× faster — while grammar converges early for all optimizers and is not the bottleneck. The mechanistic explanation is grounded in the optimizer update rules: Muon’s spectral whitening enables broad exploration of the motif landscape, while Adam’s adaptive scaling preserves co-occurrence anisotropies.

These are not artifacts of our architecture. They are mathematical properties of the optimizers that should hold wherever the same tension between first-order feature coverage and second-order relational structure exists — which is to say, across genomic foundation models broadly.

Optimizer choice in genomic AI is not merely an engineering decision about training speed. It is a modeling decision that shapes which aspects of biological structure the model learns fastest and best. Biological evaluation is necessary because loss alone cannot reveal these differences.
