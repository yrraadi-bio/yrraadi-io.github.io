Does Muon improve regulatory DNA learning? Part 2.

March 2026 · Akshat Singh, Viraj Doshi

  • MuonW achieves the highest peak motif recovery (rate correlation 0.824) and most diverse generation (1,689 unique TF profiles, 80% of reference) at high learning rates.

In Part 1, we showed that MuonW reaches target validation perplexity with roughly 37% fewer FLOPs than AdamH. We swept five learning rates across four optimizer configurations and compared convergence speed, spectral dynamics, and training stability.

But perplexity measures how well a model predicts the next nucleotide. It does not measure whether the model has learned the regulatory grammar that makes enhancers function in cells. In Part 2, we run the same 20-configuration sweep — but instead of measuring loss, we measure biology.

The Loss–Biology Gap

All four optimizer configurations converge to nearly identical training loss (~1.20 per nucleotide) after 20 epochs. Yet when we evaluate the generated sequences against a comprehensive biological quality suite, they produce substantially different results.

Why should identical loss produce different biology? The cross-entropy loss averages over all positions equally, but regulatory function concentrates in the ~10–20bp motif cores that occupy <10% of the sequence. A model that predicts flanking nucleotides perfectly but misses the binding site grammar can still achieve low per-position loss.

The gap between “good at predicting nucleotides” and “good at generating functional regulatory elements” motivates everything that follows. To characterize it properly, we need to sweep the same hyperparameter grid from Part 1 — but evaluate on biology.

The Evaluation Suite

We developed a six-axis evaluation framework, each axis targeting a distinct aspect of regulatory biology:

TF binding grammar measures whether generated sequences preserve the combinatorial logic of transcription factor co-occurrence. We quantify this using the Frobenius distance between reference and generated co-occurrence matrices, and test structural similarity with a Mantel permutation test (10,000 permutations).

Motif abundance and fidelity measures how many TF binding sites are preserved and whether their relative frequencies match the reference. We scan all 16,249 sequences per condition against 22 JASPAR vertebrate motif matrices, computing per-TF hit rates, aggregate cosine similarity, and per-sequence Jensen-Shannon divergence.

Predicted transcriptional activity uses the Malinois MPRA model1Malinois is trained on real MPRA data and predicts log2 fold-change of transcriptional activity from sequence alone. to predict whether generated sequences would drive gene expression in K562, HepG2, and SK-N-SH cell lines.

Cell-type specificity tests whether sequences generated with a K562 prompt contain K562-appropriate TFs and not hepatocyte TFs, using Fisher’s exact tests.

Regulatory element type fidelity tests whether generated promoters look like promoters (high CpG O/E) and enhancers look like enhancers (cell-type-specific TFs).

Sequence diversity measures mode collapse: unique TF binding profiles, pairwise Jaccard similarity, and k-mer diversity.

We evaluate all 20 configurations (4 optimizers × 5 learning rates) plus the reference dataset. All generations use temperature 0.5.

Biological Learning Rate Sweep

In Part 1, we swept learning rates and plotted perplexity vs LR for each optimizer. Here we do the same, but for biological quality metrics. Each point represents a fully trained model evaluated on the complete biological suite.

Several patterns stand out immediately:

  • MuonW scales with LR on motif abundance. Its rate correlation climbs from 0.621 at lr = \(9 \times 10^{-5}\) to 0.824 at lr = \(3.6 \times 10^{-4}\), and its unique TF profiles nearly double from 928 to 1,689 at the highest LR. This mirrors Part 1’s finding that MuonW “favors scale.”
  • AdamH peaks in the mid-range for grammar. Its Mantel r reaches 0.775 at lr = \(5.7 \times 10^{-3}\), then falls at higher rates — consistent with Part 1’s observation that Hyperball destabilizes at high LR.
  • MuonH collapses at high LR. Unique TF profiles drop from 1,280 to 571 between lr = \(5.7 \times 10^{-3}\) and \(2.3 \times 10^{-2}\), and grammar deteriorates sharply. This is the biological consequence of the spectral squeeze predicted in Part 1.
  • AdamW is flat. Its biological metrics vary least across the LR range. It never achieves the peak performance of the other optimizers, but it never catastrophically fails either.

The heatmaps reveal that the best optimizer depends on which biological dimension you care about — and that learning rate sensitivity varies by optimizer. AdamH is concentrated: its peak performance is narrow but high. MuonW is expansive: it achieves competitive results across a wider LR range for motif metrics, with the absolute best results at the extremes.

Threshold Analysis: Efficiency Across the Sweep

In Part 1, we measured FLOP efficiency using time-to-target: how many FLOPs to reach a perplexity of 2.51. Here we apply the same logic to biology. Instead of asking “which optimizer reaches the best score,” we ask: how many learning rates achieve a given biological quality threshold?

An optimizer that clears a threshold at 4 out of 5 learning rates is more efficient than one that only clears it at 1 — you spend fewer runs on hyperparameter search.

The threshold analysis makes the efficiency story concrete:

  • For grammar (Mantel r > 0.60): AdamH clears this at 4/5 LRs. MuonW at 2/5. AdamW at 2/5. MuonH at 1/5. If you need reliable grammar preservation, AdamH gets you there with far less tuning.
  • For motif fidelity (rate correlation > 0.70): MuonW clears this at 3/5 LRs. AdamH at 2/5. MuonH at 1/5. AdamW at 0/5. MuonW is the most efficient path to high motif recovery.
  • For diversity (>1400 profiles): MuonW and AdamH each clear this at 2/5 LRs. AdamW at 2/5. Only MuonW reaches >1500.

The peak-vs-mean comparison captures the key trade-off: MuonW has the highest peak on motif correlation and diversity, but large variance. AdamH has the highest peak on grammar, with moderate variance. AdamW has lower peaks but the tightest distribution — it wastes the fewest FLOPs on suboptimal runs.

TF Grammar: Where Adam Outperforms

The most surprising result from the sweep is AdamH’s dominance on TF grammar. Despite Muon’s advantages in motif abundance, AdamH preserves the combinatorial structure of transcription factor co-occurrence significantly better.

The interaction between gradient processing and weight normalization is asymmetric:

Why might Adam preserve grammar better? TF co-occurrence is not near-uniform — some pairs co-occur at 20× the rate of others. In Part 1, we argued Adam’s per-coordinate scaling provides “negligible benefit” for individual nucleotide prediction. But for the heterogeneous feature interaction landscape of TF grammar, coordinate-wise adaptation may capture exactly the right anisotropies. Muon’s whitening equalizes all directions — optimal for exploring the motif vocabulary, but potentially flattening the relational structure.

Spectral whitening ≠ grammar

This updates Part 1’s narrative. Muon produces spectrally white weights (entropy 0.955 vs 0.743 for AdamW) — that is factually correct. But the implicit assumption that whitening captures the combinatorial grammar of the genome is not supported. The model that learns the most words doesn’t necessarily learn the best sentences.

The Diversity Paradox

Generated sequences have higher k-mer diversity than the reference (87–95% of 10-mers vs 77%). Yet at the functional level, they are less diverse (1,200–1,700 unique TF profiles vs 2,108). This is superficial diversity without functional variety — real regulatory elements are structured, and functional motifs create repetitive k-mer patterns that lower diversity. MuonW comes closest to closing the functional diversity gap (80% of reference).

What the Weights Tell Us: CKA

Gradient processing determines what the model learns internally. Weight normalization modulates how that representation maps to biological output. This explains the asymmetric interaction: Muon’s representation is “locked in” by the whitening — Hyperball can’t reshape it. Adam’s representation is more malleable, and Hyperball reshapes it in a way that preserves relational structure.

The MuonH Cautionary Tale

MuonH at lr = \(2.27 \times 10^{-2}\) produces only 571 unique TF profiles (27% of reference) and condition numbers reaching \(2.64 \times 10^{10}\). The Malinois MPRA model predicts uniformly high, non-specific transcriptional activity — sequences that score as generically “active” without encoding any cell-type-specific program.

This is the biological consequence of the spectral squeeze described in Part 1. The effective learning rate for the up_proj layer reaches ~0.052 on the unit hypersphere, causing rank collapse and repetitive, non-specific generation.

Temperature

Sampling temperature has a large effect on biological quality. Dropping from 1.0 to 0.5 improves all metrics across all optimizers. However, the effect is not uniform: Adam variants benefit disproportionately (+57–79%) compared to Muon (+26–32%). This connects to Part 1’s activation hacking observation — MuonW’s growing weight norms effectively reduce softmax temperature during training, so explicit temperature reduction at generation has less additional effect.

Open Questions

Does grammar translate to function? Our grammar metrics measure TF co-occurrence from motif scanning. The ultimate test is whether AdamH-generated sequences show higher activity in reporter assays.

Can we get the best of both? MuonW excels at abundance; AdamH excels at grammar. A hybrid training schedule — Muon for early exploration, Adam for late-stage refinement — might capture both advantages.

Is the activation hacking real? MuonW’s growing weight norms blur genuine learning and implicit temperature reduction. A controlled experiment with fixed output-head norms would isolate this effect.

Does this generalize? Our results are for a single architecture (Axis, ~85M parameters). The optimizer × biology interaction may differ for other architectures or genomic contexts.

Summary

Part 1 showed that Muon is faster. Part 2 shows that faster does not automatically mean biologically better — but it does mean biologically different.

Across the full 20-configuration sweep, each optimizer carves out a distinct niche. MuonW produces the most motif-rich and diverse sequences, scaling with learning rate just as it scales with FLOPs. AdamH preserves combinatorial TF grammar best, achieving 22% higher Mantel correlation at its optimal LR. AdamW is the most reliable baseline — never the best, but never catastrophically bad. MuonH is high-risk: it can match or exceed the others in narrow regimes, but collapses at high learning rates.

The most important finding is that spectral entropy — the “whiteness” that Muon’s proponents highlight — does not correlate with grammar quality. The axes of biological quality are not aligned with the axes of weight matrix geometry. For practitioners: match the optimizer to the biological objective. For researchers: optimizer design for genomic foundation models deserves more attention than it currently receives.
