What's Actually in Your Training Data?

March 2, 2026 · Aditya Sivakumar

Highlights

  • 28 clean axes of regulatory variation survive permutation testing across 2.3M cCREs and 3,071 bulk samples.
  • TF motif specificity spans from highly cell-type-restricted (NEUROD2, Gini = 0.40) to ubiquitous housekeeping factors (SP4, Gini = 0.05).
  • Block-diagonal lineage structure recapitulates developmental biology from chromatin alone: within-lineage JSD of 0.003–0.019 vs cross-lineage 0.20–0.30+.
  • 14 of 87 specific TFs reproduced across independent bulk and single-cell pipelines, confirming the signal is real.

We’re building models that design synthetic regulatory DNA: short sequences that control which genes turn on in which cell types. The goal is cell-type-specific gene therapy, where a therapeutic transgene expresses only in the cells that need it. The input to these models is a library of cis-regulatory elements (cCREs), including enhancers, promoters, and silencers, the non-coding sequences that control cell-type-specific gene expression.1What is a cCRE? Candidate cis-Regulatory Elements are non-coding genomic regions identified by the ENCODE project’s SCREEN pipeline from DNase hypersensitivity signal. They are classified as dELS (distal enhancer-like), pELS (proximal enhancer-like), CTCF-only, or PLS (promoter-like) based on their chromatin signatures. Every cell in your body has the same ~3 billion base-pair genome, but a cortical neuron and a liver hepatocyte express completely different genes. The difference is which cCREs are open.

But a generative model trained on cCREs is only as good as the data it sees. Batch artifacts, technical noise, poor cell-type resolution: any of these mean the model learns nothing useful. So before anything touches a model, we put the library through a series of checks. This post walks through what we tested and what we found.

The Data Landscape

We started with two datasets that both measure chromatin accessibility, but at very different scales.

The first is ENCODE’s bulk DNase-seq catalog: 2.3 million cCREs across 3,071 human samples from every major organ system. DNase-seq finds regions where the chromatin is open, unwound from its histone packaging and accessible to transcription factors.2How DNase-seq works. DNase I preferentially cleaves nucleosome-free DNA; sequencing the cut sites reveals regions of open chromatin. This is a tissue-level assay: the signal averages across all cell types in the sample. The breadth is good (thousands of samples across every organ), but each sample is a blend of cell types ground together. You can see what regulatory elements exist across the human body. You can’t attribute them to individual cell types.

The second dataset gives us cell-type resolution. The Allen Brain Institute’s snATAC-seq atlas profiles ~9.4 million individual nuclei from 130 samples spanning 42+ brain regions and 106 cell types. For each individual cell, we know which cCREs are open.3How snATAC-seq differs from bulk. Each nucleus is individually barcoded via combinatorial indexing or droplet-based capture, so chromatin accessibility is measured per-cell rather than averaged across tissue. The cost is extreme sparsity: most regions read as “0” in any single cell because only ~10,000 of ~2 million possible regions are captured per nucleus. The tradeoff is scope: this atlas covers the brain in depth, but only the brain.

Together, bulk gives us breadth and statistical power; single-cell gives us cell-type resolution. The question is whether they agree, and whether either one contains real regulatory signal at all.

Separating Signal from Noise

The first question is the most basic: is the variation in this dataset biological, or is it just technical noise?

We ran PCA on the full bulk accessibility matrix (2.3 million cCREs across 3,071 samples). Early PCs were interpretable: PC1 (15.0% of variance) separates chromatin compaction from transcriptional activity. PC2 (8.3%) separates brain from blood, with immune signaling on one end and lipid metabolism on the other (η² = 0.77 for tissue type). PC14 separates cortex from basal ganglia.

But you can always find patterns in data. The question is whether the later PCs are above what you’d get from chance structure. We shuffled sample labels 100 times, re-ran PCA each time, and built a null distribution for the variance explained by each component. Thirty PCs exceed the null (all \(p = 0.010\)). Two of those turned out to be batch: PC3 correlates with file size (\(r = -0.52\), a proxy for sequencing depth4Why file size is a batch proxy. Larger files reflect deeper sequencing, which systematically inflates accessibility estimates in samples with more reads. When a PC correlates with file size, it is capturing technical variance in sequencing depth rather than biological variation.), PC6 with experimental batch (\(r = -0.38\)). We dropped both.

That leaves 28 clean axes of biological variation. By PC30 (the last one above the null) the variance explained has dropped from 41% to 0.14%, but it still clears the permutation threshold with a z-score of 11.7. Past that, noise.

What the axes actually encode

Permutation testing tells you the axes are real. It doesn’t tell you what they mean. For that, we looked at the specific cCREs with the highest loadings on each PC. If PC2 really separates brain from blood, the cCREs driving it should sit on genes with known brain or blood functions, and ideally be promoters near the transcription start site.

On the brain side of PC2 (8.3% of variance), the highest-loading promoter-like cCREs include:

  • GRM3 (glutamate metabotropic receptor 3). PLS element 88 bp from its TSS. Receptor for the brain’s primary excitatory neurotransmitter.
  • NTM (neurotrimin), a neural cell adhesion molecule involved in neurite outgrowth. Promoter 87 bp away.
  • CX3CL1 (fractalkine), the primary neuron-to-microglia signaling molecule. Promoter 36 bp away.
  • GPR75, a G-protein coupled receptor enriched in brain. Promoter 7 bp from the TSS, essentially overlapping it.
  • AQP4 (aquaporin-4), the water channel at astrocyte endfeet along the blood–brain barrier. Promoter 2.5 kb away.

On the blood side of the same axis, the top cCREs are proximal enhancers near hematopoietic genes: MYO1G (a myosin expressed exclusively in blood cells, pELS 1.6 kb), ADAM8 (a metalloprotease involved in leukocyte migration, pELS 1.8 kb), and ITGB7 (integrin beta-7, leukocyte homing, PLS 21 bp).

So the axis separating brain from blood in PCA is built from promoters of glutamate receptors on one side and leukocyte integrins on the other.

The same pattern holds for other axes. PC1 (chromatin compaction vs transcriptional activity, 15.0%) has high-loading promoters near FOSL2 (AP-1 transcription factor, PLS 4.4 kb) and NR2F2/COUP-TFII (nuclear receptor controlling tissue-specific enhancer activity, PLS 1.5 kb), both involved in chromatin state regulation. PC14 (cortex vs basal ganglia) loads on SYT3 (synaptotagmin-3, enriched in subcortical structures, pELS 560 bp on the basal ganglia side) and TAGLN (transgelin, a smooth-muscle marker enriched in cortical vasculature, pELS 210 bp on the cortical side).

We ran the same analysis on a brain-only PCA (813 brain samples, separate from the all-ENCODE decomposition). The cerebellum axis (brain PC2, 15.4% of variance, Cohen’s \(d = 6.04\)) loads on promoters of LHX1 (PLS 33 bp from its TSS), a LIM-homeodomain factor required for cerebellar Purkinje cell differentiation, and BARHL2 (pELS 801 bp), which specifies cerebellar granule neurons. The basal ganglia axis (brain PC4, \(d = 1.55\)) loads on PPP1R1B, better known as DARPP-32, at 456 bp from its nearest pELS. DARPP-32 is the canonical marker of medium spiny neurons in the striatum. The hypothalamus/hippocampus axis (brain PC23) loads on FOXG1 (telencephalon identity, PLS 2.4 kb) on the cortical side and IRX3 (iroquois homeobox 3, involved in hypothalamic energy-balance circuits, PLS 103 bp) on the hypothalamic side.

Does the Library Encode Cell Identity?

The bulk data contains real biological variation, but the more important question is whether the library encodes cell-type-specific regulatory programs. For that, we need the single-cell data.

We scanned 981 deduplicated TF binding motifs across all 106 brain cell types and computed a Gini coefficient for each, measuring how unevenly a motif is distributed. Gini of 0: equally enriched everywhere. Gini near 1: concentrated in one cell type.5What TF families mean. bHLH (basic helix-loop-helix) factors are the master regulators of neural cell fate; they form homo- and heterodimers that bind E-box motifs (CANNTG) in enhancers. AP-1 factors (FOS, JUN, JUNB) form dimers that mediate activity-dependent gene expression. KLF/SP factors regulate broadly expressed metabolic and chromatin genes.

The most specific TFs are the ones a developmental neurobiologist would predict:

  • NEUROD2 (Gini = 0.40), a bHLH factor that drives cortical neuron maturation in layers 4/5, activating genes for synaptic transmission and dendritic growth. In our data, it’s restricted almost entirely to excitatory neuron subtypes.
  • OLIG1/2/3 (Gini = 0.34–0.40), the oligodendrocyte lineage specification factors. OLIG2 is required for both oligodendrocyte precursors and motor neuron progenitors; OLIG1 drives terminal differentiation. Their specificity means the library resolves glial from neuronal programs.
  • NEUROG1 (Gini = 0.33), a proneural factor specifying glutamatergic fate from radial glia progenitors during cortical neurogenesis. This is a developmental factor, which means the library captures regulatory logic from differentiation, not just mature cell states.

At the other end: SP4 (Gini = 0.18), a zinc-finger factor that broadly regulates neuronal transcription, and EGR1 (Gini = 0.18), an immediate-early gene that fires in any active neuron. These are housekeeping programs, not cell identity markers.

One detail on curation: these 981 motifs aren’t the raw JASPAR output. We removed 138 whose parent TF isn’t expressed in brain (HPA nTPM < 1.0) and deduplicated 653 with correlated binding profiles (complete-linkage \(r > 0.95\)). That leaves 190 non-redundant, brain-expressed motifs (19% of the original set). Every surviving motif corresponds to a protein actually present in brain tissue.

Gini coefficient formula

The Gini coefficient measures inequality of a motif’s hit rate across cell types. For a motif with hit rates \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\) across \(n\) cell types:

\[ G = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}|x_i - x_j|}{2n\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i} \]

\(G = 0\) means the motif is equally enriched in every cell type (completely ubiquitous); \(G \to 1\) means it is concentrated in a single cell type (perfectly specific).

The Lineage Test

Individual motif specificity is useful, but we also need to check whether the relationships between cell types make sense. If the library encodes real regulatory biology, cell types from the same developmental lineage should look more similar to each other than to unrelated types.6Lineage in this context. Brain cells derive from neural stem cells that progressively restrict their fate. The major branch point separates neurons (excitatory + inhibitory) from glia (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes). Within neurons, excitatory cells (glutamatergic, born in the cortex) and inhibitory cells (GABAergic, born in the ganglionic eminences) have distinct developmental origins despite intermingling in the mature brain.

We tested this two ways. First, the Jaccard index: for any pair of cell types, what fraction of their cCREs overlap? Within the oligodendrocyte lineage (OGC_1 through OPC and COP), subtypes share 77–87% of their open chromatin regions. These cells all descend from a common precursor, and even as they mature they retain most of their ancestor’s regulatory landscape. Oligodendrocytes vs excitatory neurons: overlap drops to 0.15–0.25. Those lineages diverged early in development (ventral progenitors vs dorsal radial glia).

Second, Jensen–Shannon divergence, which measures whether two cell types use the same transcription factor binding motifs, not just whether they share the same open regions. Within-lineage JSD ranges from 0.003–0.019: cells in the same lineage use the same regulatory programs. Cross-lineage JSD reaches 0.20–0.30+.

The heatmaps below show clear block-diagonal structure: tight clusters within each lineage, sharp boundaries between them. Excitatory neurons, inhibitory subtypes (PVALB, SST, VIP, LAMP5, SNCG), astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia. This structure was not imposed by our analysis; no labels were used in the clustering. It falls out of the data from unsupervised hierarchical clustering alone.

Mathematical definitions

Jaccard index. For two cell types \(A\) and \(B\) with sets of accessible cCREs:

\[ J(A,B) = \frac{|A \cap B|}{|A \cup B|} \]

\(J = 1\) means identical regulatory landscapes; \(J = 0\) means completely disjoint.

Jensen–Shannon divergence. For TF motif distributions \(P\) and \(Q\) over a shared motif vocabulary:

\[ \text{JSD}(P \| Q) = \frac{1}{2}D_{\text{KL}}(P \| M) + \frac{1}{2}D_{\text{KL}}(Q \| M) \]

where \(M = \frac{1}{2}(P + Q)\) and

\[ D_{\text{KL}}(P \| M) = \sum_i P(i)\log\frac{P(i)}{M(i)} \]

JSD is symmetric and bounded in \([0, \ln 2]\). Values near 0 mean identical TF programs; large values mean divergent regulatory grammars.

Do the Two Pipelines Agree?

The two pipelines above use different assays, different samples, different statistical frameworks, and different definitions of “specificity.” Comparing their outputs is a direct test of whether the signal is real.

Of 87 TFs flagged as significantly specific by either pipeline, 14 appear in both. The bar is high: a TF has to be specific enough to emerge at single-cell resolution among 106 cell types and variable enough to show up in tissue-level PCA across 3,071 bulk samples. The 14 include NEUROD2 and OLIG3 (top cell-type factors from the Gini analysis), TCF21 (mesenchymal lineage), TFAP2E (cerebellar/olfactory), and several AP-1 family members (JUNB, JUND, FOSL2) marking medium spiny neurons of the striatum.

The non-overlapping TFs are informative too. The bulk-only TFs (34) are dominated by immediate-early genes (FOSB, JUN, EGR2/3/4) and metabolic regulators (KLF1/7/11/14/15). These vary between brain regions (different activity levels) but don’t distinguish individual cell types within a region.

The single-cell-only TFs (64) are cell-fate determinants invisible to bulk because they distinguish cell types within the same tissue. DLX5 specifies forebrain inhibitory neurons. FOXG1 defines telencephalon identity. LHX6 guides MGE-derived interneuron migration. NEUROG2 determines cortical excitatory fate. These only become visible at single-cell resolution.7Why this split makes biological sense. Bulk tissue is a mixture of cell types, so the TFs that emerge from bulk analysis are those that vary between brain regions (different mixtures of cell types) rather than between individual cell types. Activity-dependent genes (FOS, JUN, EGR family) fire in all active neurons and are prominent in tissue with high neural activity. Single-cell analysis resolves individual cells, so the TFs that emerge are those that distinguish one cell type from another within the same tissue: developmental fate determinants.

The Hardest Test

Everything above benefits from large sample sizes (thousands of bulk samples, hundreds of thousands of cells). The harder question is whether the framework holds up when data is scarce. We zoomed in to individual brain regions and asked: how many differential cCREs does each one produce, and do the TFs that emerge make biological sense?

The range is wide. The cerebellum, developmentally distinct from the rest of the brain and born from the rhombic lip rather than the forebrain, produces 38,360 differential cCREs. The basal ganglia yield 1,010. The cortex, 1,309. Then you get to the underrepresented regions: the hypothalamus yields 27 differential cCREs. The amygdala, 2. And the substantia nigra, home to the dopaminergic neurons whose death causes Parkinson’s disease, produces just 256 (134 enriched, 122 depleted).

Those 134 enriched cCREs in the substantia nigra are enough to resolve 15 unique brain-relevant TFs, transcription factors that appear in no other brain region’s enriched set. The top hits include NR2F1, a nuclear receptor that regulates cortical area identity (142 nTPM in the region). ZBTB18, a zinc-finger repressor critical for cortical neuron migration, shows the highest expression of any hit at 462 nTPM. RORA, a retinoid-related orphan receptor involved in cerebellar and cortical development, at 153 nTPM. NR4A1, a nuclear receptor in the NURR1 family important for dopaminergic neuron survival, at 288 nTPM. All of these are known regulators of neural programs consistent with this region’s neuronal identity.8Expression validation. nTPM (normalized Transcripts Per Million) values come from the Human Protein Atlas snRNA-seq data for each brain region. Requiring nTPM ≥ 1.0 ensures that every TF we report is not just computationally enriched but actually expressed as protein in the tissue.

Across all 8 brain regions, distinct TF signatures emerge. The cerebellum is dominated by unique developmental programs consistent with its separate embryonic origin. The basal ganglia show enrichment for AP-1 and activity-dependent factors. And even at the very bottom of data availability, the substantia nigra produces biologically coherent results.

The cases where gene therapy is most needed (rare diseases, understudied cell populations, small patient cohorts) are exactly the cases where data is hardest to come by. Extracting meaningful regulatory signatures from 134 cCREs in the substantia nigra suggests the framework is applicable in those settings.

What This Means

The bulk data contains 28 clean axes of regulatory variation that survive permutation testing, and the cCREs driving those axes sit on promoters of genes with known functions matching each axis’s biology. The single-cell data resolves TF motifs into a spectrum from cell-type-specific fate determinants to ubiquitous housekeeping. The relationships between cell types recapitulate developmental lineage from unsupervised clustering alone. And the strongest signals replicate across two independent pipelines.

The result is a cCRE library where the regulatory structure has been verified at every level we could test: individual motifs, pairwise relationships, lineage architecture, cross-modal reproducibility, and small-sample robustness. When we train generative models on this library to design synthetic regulatory DNA, we know the patterns they’re learning reflect biology.
